## Capability vs. action

How can you have two people who seem so radically different in their abilities? One seems to have superior talent, skill, education — and yet doesn't produce the quality of life they want. Meanwhile, someone with every disadvantage — no emotional support, no family support, no education, no background — goes out and produces results way beyond what anyone could expect.

The difference in the quality of our lives does not come down to capability. What human beings are *capable* of is incredible. What people *will* do is rather different from what they *can* do. What I'm going to challenge you to do is start exercising your will — to start using what I call your **personal power.** Personal power means the ability to act, to take action and produce results. It's a power you already have. It's not something I'm going to give you; it's something that will hopefully awaken within you — in two ways: by igniting your desire, and by showing you simple, systematic strategies to get greater results out of yourself daily.

When most people think of success or failure, they think of big, giant things. But failure is not an overnight experience, and neither is success. Failure is the result of failing to make the calls, failing to follow through, failing to say "I love you," failing to give 100%. Success is also one step at a time: successfully making the call, successfully following through, successfully breaking through the limits that used to stop you. It's the culmination of all those little successes over a 30-day period — and over a lifetime — that creates a life you take total pride and joy in knowing is your own.

I have incredible respect for you, and I care about you, even though I haven't met you. You and I are kindred spirits: you've picked up this program, you've made an investment, and you're listening to it. Power comes from concentrating your focus and taking daily action to improve something. If all you do is work on something every day and improve 1% a day, what's the difference within a year? It's not 365%, because 1% a day starts stacking on top of itself. We're talking about differences way beyond anything you can imagine. By making simple, small changes daily, you can absolutely transform the quality of your life.

The problem is that most of us do not control the power of our mental focus. Most of us focus on things like, "How come my life isn't working? How come this always happens to me?" Most people major in minor things — focused on how to make a living instead of how to design a life. These 30 days are a study of the majors: how to take care of yourself mentally, emotionally, physically, and financially.

## Why I studied success

What drove me to study success at this depth? The same thing that drives all of us: one of two experiences — inspiration or desperation. For me, it was desperation.

I grew up in an environment I didn't like. I saw my father work extremely hard as a salesman, 40 or 50 hours a week, and yet most of the time we were broke, and I didn't understand why. I remember one Thanksgiving when there was no money for food, and a service organization delivered food to our house. My dad didn't want to accept it but felt he had to, and I remember what that did to him emotionally. That drove enough pain inside me that I decided: I never want my family to go through this, and I never want to go through it myself. Someday I'm going to master financial success — not just to help myself, but the people around me.

In high school I wasn't the most popular kid, but I loved people and wanted everybody to like me. I thought, "Someday I'll meet the most popular kid in school and find out what makes him so well-liked. He's probably one of the warmest, most genuine people you could meet." The day I met him, he treated me like dirt — like how dare I enter his space. And I thought: how could someone this rude be so successful at getting people to like him? It made me want to study what makes people like other people.

I looked at couples who'd been married 5, 10, 15 years and still had passion and drive for each other — still loved each other 100%, like newlyweds — and I wondered how they could last 15 years when most people can't last 15 months. And one day I asked my mother, "How come this is my fourth father?" Her explanation motivated me to want to make relationships work.

So I said: I don't like the way my life is, and I want to make it better. Somewhere in my mind I linked up that what I should do is **study success.** If you want anything in life, you have to make it a study. If you want to be successful, study it. If you want to be happy, study happiness. Wealthy — study wealth. Healthy — study health. You can't just hope it turns out okay; you can see what hoping does for most people.

So I started studying success like a madman. I read almost 700 books in human development — everything from basic positive thinking to more esoteric forms of change. I listened to tapes constantly in my car. I went to seminar after seminar; many were lousy, but my attitude was: if I get one idea, it's worth it. And if all a seminar does is tell me what I already know — well, by hearing it again, maybe this time I'll actually *use* it. **Repetition is the mother of skill.** I poured so much good stuff in that, eventually, good stuff started coming back out.

## The success — and the self-sabotage

Within a short time I became successful. My definition of success back then, growing up poor, was money. I figured if you make enough money it'll solve all of life's problems. Is that true? Of course not — but at least you can arrive at your problems in style.

By 19 I thought I'd made it. I used massive action, applied everything I read, and built a big company. Newspapers and magazines wrote me up as a young entrepreneur; they called me "Wonder Boy." I was making about $10,000 a month — for a 19-year-old, that seemed like a lot of success.

But I'd never had that level of success before — that much money, that many people acknowledging me. It was beyond my comfort zone. Consciously I thought I wanted more; but subconsciously, most of us have an idea of what we think we deserve. Imagine a **thermostat** attached to your brain. Set it at 68 degrees: if the room drops to 62, the heater kicks on — your brain says, "This isn't good enough," and you get a push to change things. But it works the other way too: if things heat up to 72 or 73, the air conditioner kicks on, and you begin to **sabotage your own success**, dropping back to where you subconsciously think you deserve to be — in your relationships, your finances, your level of acknowledgment. It doesn't make sense intellectually, but it makes sense when you study how human beings behave.

And that's what I did — I began to sabotage. I stopped showing up for key meetings. I treated people harshly who didn't deserve it. I'd never done drugs or alcohol, but I used food like a drug: if I didn't like how I felt, I'd eat, then look in the mirror, feel worse, and eat more. Within two and a half months I gained 38 pounds — which isn't easy to do. As I sabotaged, it spread to everyone around me. I fired almost everyone in my company. I went totally broke and moved into a 400-square-foot bachelor apartment in Venice, California — no kitchen, washing my dishes in the bathtub — waking up each morning with major goals like, "What am I going to eat today, and what's going to happen to Luke and Laura on *General Hospital*?"

## The Ultimate Success Formula

Here's the message of this first day: **what changes your life is not learning more — it's making decisions, using your personal power, and taking action.** Look up *power* in the dictionary: the ability to act. That's what sets people's lives apart.

To turn my life around I used what I call the **Ultimate Success Formula.** It has four steps.

**Step one: know your outcome.** Decide what you want. The clearer you are, the more you empower your brain to come up with answers. **Clarity is power.** This works in any moment — say you're in an argument and you're so caught up that you've forgotten what you're arguing for, but you know you have to win. If you stop and ask, "What's my outcome here?" you'll usually find your outcome isn't to argue — it's to resolve something. Get in the habit of asking, "What's my outcome?" Even on your way to visit a friend: maybe the outcome is just to have fun, or to make your friend feel appreciated and loved. Knowing that raises your odds a thousandfold versus just showing up and wondering why you're bored.

**Step two: take action.** Get yourself to move toward your goals. People say, "But I don't know what to do. What if I try and it doesn't work?" If you try and it doesn't work, you got an education — you're better off than before, because now you know what doesn't work. Most people never even know what they want; and a few who do, do nothing about it — they sit around and say, "Someday, honey, we'll do this." Someday is now. The road to *someday* leads to a town of *nowhere.* How do you get yourself to take action? You **decide.** The word *decision* comes from a Latin root meaning **to cut off from** — when you decide, you cut off from any other possibility. The biggest trap that keeps people from acting is fear: fear of failure, success, rejection, pain, the unknown. The only way to deal with fear is to face it and take action in spite of it. (At my seminars, people end by walking across a bed of hot coals — not to learn picnic skills, and not "mind over matter." You could do it right now; I'm not encouraging you to, but it's possible. You could also be up at 6 a.m. with total passion, have the person of your dreams in your life, and be earning what you're worth — but few people do, because it's not what we *can* do that matters, it's what we *will* do.) Fear can serve us — it makes us pay attention — as long as fear is a *counselor and not a jailer.* Breaking through fear is like a muscle: start small, build daily, and soon what felt impossible becomes effortless.

**Step three: notice what you're getting.** Once you take action, do your actions always work? Of course not. The most successful people have failed more than anybody else. In baseball, a Hall-of-Famer fails about 7 times out of 10 — they succeed only 30% of the time. That's how life is too. So develop **sensory acuity:** pay close attention to whether you're moving toward your goal or away from it. (Have you ever been polite to someone going on and on, and they mistook your politeness for interest? Or thought someone was interested when they were just being polite? That's a failure to notice.) Don't kid yourself and keep doing something just because you've worked hard at it. Know what you're getting.

**Step four: change your approach.** If what you're doing isn't working, don't feel like a failure — just change your approach. Try something else. "But what if that doesn't work?" Try something else. "And if that doesn't work?" Try something else. How long do you keep changing? Until you succeed. How long would you give a baby to learn to walk? You'd let them keep trying until they walk — magic formula, no wonder almost everyone walks. But adults have learned to fear failure, so after a couple of tries they quit, because not looking good would be painful. Break out of that. **Flexibility is power.**

Think of a plane flying from the mainland to Hawaii: it's off course about 95% of the time. The crew knows their outcome (where they're going), they fire up the engines and take action, and a computer tells them whether they're on course or off. They don't panic when they're off course — they simply notice, adjust, and keep correcting back and forth until they land in Hawaii on a dime. That's the Ultimate Success Formula.

## Welcome frustration

Break through the fears that stop you — and the biggest one is failure. Failure can be your best friend. The greatest successes are often people who had so much pain from a failure that it drove them to greater success. Those little Post-it Notes? They came from a chemist who was trying to create a permanent adhesive and instead made one that barely stuck — a complete "failure" — but he asked, "What could I use this for?" and turned it into a multimillion-dollar business.

Lee Iacocca is one of the most admired role models in our country. He worked his way up through Ford, driven, willing to try almost anything, refusing to take no for an answer — and then he had a run-in with Henry Ford, who fired him, and it burned him badly. When Chrysler came calling — a company that looked impossible to turn around — do you think he'd have taken it if he hadn't been fired? According to his wife, he'd said, "Honey, I don't think anybody could turn this thing around," and she said, "Boy, will Mr. Ford be happy to hear that." That's all it took. He was so fueled by the pain of that "failure" that he had to prove Ford made the mistake. IBM started much the same way: Tom Watson got fired by the CEO of a competitor (NCR), got so angry he said, "I'm going to build a company that will dwarf theirs someday" — and did.

So welcome frustration. Over the next 30 days you may feel frustrated or upset, like it's not worth the effort. Commit and follow through. When I get frustrated, I've conditioned myself to get *excited*, because frustration means I'm about to have a breakthrough — my brain is searching. When I'm confused, instead of "I'll never learn this," my response is, "I'm about to learn something." The emotions many of us label negative — frustration, rejection, pain — can be our greatest friends, because they drive us to success.

One trap: when people get successful, they often get superstitious. They worked so hard for it that they stop changing, stop varying their behavior, get conservative — and miss the juice of life. When people succeed, they tend to party; when they fail, they tend to ponder — and it's in the pondering that we often create our greatest successes. Just remember: **it's impossible to fail.** If you try something and it doesn't work, try something else and learn from it. As long as you learn something, you've succeeded.
