# Day 19 · Conquering Fear of Failure, Success & Rejection

> 📄 **[Read the full cleaned transcript →](../transcripts/day-19.md)**  ·  🏠 **[Home](../README.md)**

**Core message:** Fear is just an emotional state — your brain activating to avoid pain — and it only becomes a jailer when you attach it to harmless things like "failure." Failure, success, and rejection don't exist outside you; they're labels you fire off based on *rules* you never consciously chose. Rewrite the rules so it's **hard to feel like a failure and easy to succeed**, then use conditioning techniques to scrub the old fear out at the gut level.

## The big ideas

- **Fear = an emotional state to avoid pain.** It was designed to protect you — to wake you up, sharpen attention, give access to fight-or-flight. The problem is attaching that same intensity to things that don't warrant it, like trying something and having it not turn out. We don't want to *eliminate* fear, only the fear that limits rather than supports us.
- **"FEAR = False Evidence Appearing Real."** Most fear is anticipated pain that never arrives — but your body runs the movie as if it did. "A coward dies a thousand deaths, a courageous man only one."
- **Failure is impossible — unless you label yourself.** Nothing and no one outside you can decide you're a failure or a success. To *feel* like a failure, you have to represent and evaluate your life in a specific way. You are in control.
- **It all comes down to your rules.** Ask two people what makes them a success or failure and you'll get wildly different answers (the man earning $2M/year who felt like a failure because his rule was "$4–5M"). Most rules were never consciously designed — a pattern took over and now runs you.
- **Make it hard to feel bad, easy to feel good.** Robbins flipped from "achieve, *then* allow myself to be happy" to **"happily achieve"** — and says it radically changed his personality and performance. His own rules: *if I give my all and learn something, I've succeeded; the only way to fail is to quit.*
- **Core beliefs generate emotions.** "What would you attempt to do if you knew you couldn't fail?" (a frame he credits to Robert Schuller) — set the right rules and it becomes almost impossible to fail.
- **The past does not equal the future.** Carrying old "luggage" into the present poisons current performance (Byron Scott associating *failure* every time the ball reached him).
- **The four-step change pattern** (used on failure, success *and* rejection): **1) Decide. 2) Get leverage** (link massive pain to not changing, massive pleasure to changing). **3) Interrupt the pattern** with the *erasure technique.* **4) Condition / rehearse success** with anchors.
- **Fear of success is real too.** Some link pain to succeeding — fear of being disliked, or fear that a higher standard just makes it easier to "fail" next time.
- **Rejection is the core fear behind both.** It too only exists if you generate it. *"Massive rejection is the key to success"* — if no one's rejecting you, you're probably being insignificant.

## Memorable lines & metaphors

- **"Failure is impossible. The only way you can possibly fail is to tell yourself that you have failed."**
- **"FEAR — false evidence that appears real."**
- **"I now happily achieve, instead of achieving to be happy."**
- **"What would you attempt to do if you knew you could not fail?"**
- **The football game:** if you ran a play that didn't work, you're still on the field — and "if your heart's moving, that's the clock. It's still ticking."
- **"Boom"** — the one-word anchor that put Byron Scott back on a success track.
- **The scratched record:** replay a memory backwards, weirdly, enough times and "you'll never be able to play that record the same way again."
- **Massive rejection is the key to success** — *MAS*H* turned down by 32 producers; Stallone rejected by 1,000+ agents, then offered money *not* to star in *Rocky*. "In the Rambo movies, he's not shooting up bad guys — he's shooting up all those agents that rejected him." *(Unverifiable as stated; treat as motivational lore.)*
- **Be a Buddha:** the man who traveled miles to abuse Buddha — "If I decline to accept your abuse, does it not then still belong to you?"

## The Day 19 exercise

This tape stacks several "stop the tape" assignments. The full sequence:

1. **Define your *current* rules.** Honestly write down: *What must happen for you to feel successful? What must happen for you to feel like a failure?*
2. **Interrogate them.** Whose rules are these (almost certainly not consciously yours)? And are they appropriate for lifelong happiness and success?
3. **Write two NEW definitions** — make it *easy* to feel successful and *hard* to feel like a failure (e.g. Robbins': succeed if you give your all and learn; fail only if you totally quit).
4. **Get leverage on fear of success.** In your Success Journal write (a) everything you'll *lose* if you don't change, and (b) everything you'll *gain* by linking pleasure to success — then run the painful ideas about success forwards and backwards until they're too absurd to hold.
5. **Run the *erasure technique* on a past failure.** Stand tall, breathe strong, set a positive anchor. Watch the painful memory once on a "screen" while staying good. Then, starting at the end, run it **backwards at hyperspeed** with cartoon sounds, favorite colors, Mickey-Mouse ears on the people — forward, backward, ~six times, weirder each pass — until the memory makes you grin or goes neutral.
6. **Condition success in.** Get into a peak state and rehearse succeeding over and over, locking it to an anchor (Byron's "boom").
7. **Dissolve rejection the same way:** decide; in your Success Journal write the long-term *price* of not handling rejection and the *pleasure* of being free of it; build a new rule that makes rejection genuinely hard to feel; and condition yourself so that being rejected actually *fires off strength.*
8. **The closing homework (due "Day 22").** In your Success Journal write **two past "failures" and two past "rejections."** Next to each, write a **benefit** you can extract from it (a lesson, more drive, more empathy). Then run the **erasure technique** on all four so they become *learning experiences* instead of emotional wounds.

## Make it stick — how to apply it, plus honest context

**What modern psychology backs up**

- "Fear is an emotional state activated to avoid pain" is broadly consistent with the **threat / fight-or-flight response** — and reframing it as *useful in context* mirrors **arousal-reappraisal** research (reinterpreting nerves as energy improves performance).
- "It's your *rules* and definitions, not the event" is the heart of **cognitive appraisal theory** and CBT — emotions follow from interpretation, and editing the interpretation changes the feeling.
- Setting reachable, process-based success criteria ("give my all and learn") aligns with **mastery vs. performance goals** and a **growth mindset.**
- The anchoring / "boom" trigger is **classical conditioning** (he explicitly cites Pavlov), and rehearsing success is **mental imagery / visualization**, both well supported in sport psychology.
- The erasure technique resembles NLP's "fast phobia cure"; the *general* mechanism — revisiting a memory in a calm, playful state and reducing its emotional charge — rhymes with **exposure** and **memory reconsolidation** ideas.

**Where to keep your skepticism**

- The 15-to-20-minute total cures of lifelong phobias — and especially the woman whose trichotillomania (and a history of childhood sexual abuse) reportedly shifted on a seminar stage — are **extraordinary, unverifiable claims.** Genuine trauma deserves a qualified therapist, not a stage exercise; take these as motivational anecdotes, not a treatment plan.
- The rejection stories (*MAS*H*'s 32 producers, Stallone's 1,000+ agents) are **dramatized lore** — directionally true that success involves heavy rejection, but the exact numbers aren't something to bank on.
- **"Nothing outside you can make you feel rejected"** is empowering but an overreach — it can slide into ignoring real mistreatment or feedback worth heeding. Robbins himself hedges: don't accept everything at face value, but *do* still listen.
- "Massive rejection is the key to success" rewards persistence but risks **survivorship bias** — plenty of rejected ideas were simply not good. Pair grit with honest feedback and a willingness to change approach.

**Apply it now:** Do step 1 first — write your *real* current rules for success and failure, then notice how arbitrary they are. Just rewriting them ("I succeed if I give my all and learn; I only fail if I quit") removes a surprising amount of the fear before you ever touch a technique.

**Looking ahead:** This side closes the fear trilogy — failure, success, and rejection — and points you to **Day 22 ("Till then, live with passion")**, where the homework on past failures and rejections comes due.
