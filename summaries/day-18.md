# Day 18 · End Financial Self-Sabotage

> 📄 **[Read the full cleaned transcript →](../transcripts/day-18.md)**  ·  🏠 **[Home](../README.md)**

**Core message:** The number-one thing that keeps people from financial success isn't a missing plan, advice, intelligence, or even capital — it's *self-sabotage*. Right when you start to make progress, you do something to undo it, because at some level your brain has linked **more pain than pleasure** to having money. Fix the wiring — link massive pain to *not* having abundance and massive pleasure to having it — and the sabotage disappears.

## The big ideas

- **Sabotage is the real obstacle.** Not lack of plan, advice, possibility, intelligence, or capital — it's the "unending process" of undoing your own progress (skipping key meetings, treating people harshly, distractions, overeating).
- **It traces back to pain/pleasure.** You sabotage because, consciously or subconsciously, you believe accumulating wealth will lead to more pain than pleasure. Robbins' own story: at 19, ~$10k/month, "Wonderboy" — until friends judged him, made excuses, stopped going to dinner. His brain linked *money = rejection / being an outcast*, and subconsciously destroyed everything financially.
- **The brain works by association, not logic.** To stop the sabotage you must **change your neuro-associations about money** — make earning purely positive and pleasurable, not a future source of pain (taxes, worry, the unknown).
- **Fear of the unknown.** Most people haven't *conditioned themselves emotionally* for wealth. The status quo is known and comfortable; abundance is the scary unknown. You must build the "muscle" to hold money, just as you'd condition your body with curls.
- **Step one to wealth: realize you already are wealthy.** Wealth = abundance of what you desire, plus **leverage** — experiences you didn't personally create (public highways, libraries, your breakfast you didn't hunt, salt you didn't mine). Compared to the rest of the world, our daily abundance is "absurd." *Like attracts like* — what you hold in mind is what you experience.
- **It's right to want more.** Just as it's right to grow smarter, closer to a spouse, or nearer to your creator, it's right to expand financially — *if you could have more, should you? Absolutely.*
- **Equal in opportunity, not in decisions.** We're not equal in education, motivation, or what we demand of ourselves. Robbins acknowledges real disparity (the South Bronx, poor role models) but says a few from every environment make it work — because of **empowering beliefs**, not more talent.
- **Beliefs are the governor.** Finances won't change until core beliefs change. Working harder alone fails: beliefs are like a **rubber band** that snaps you back to where you started. The fix is to expand the picture — build a "road" that always expands, and that lets others reach their dreams too.
- **Empowering beliefs to install:** money is a *measure of the value you create for others*; live in an **attitude of gratitude**; consistently focus on **giving more than you expect back** (it returns — maybe not from the same person, but from somewhere).

## Memorable lines & metaphors

- "You always end up with more month at the end of the money."
- **The rubber band:** push, push, push — then "whammo," it snaps you back to where you were.
- **The governor on your car:** old beliefs cap how far you'll go, like a speed limiter — "that's the highest you're going to go."
- **The carrot and the stick** drive you forward financially.
- **Advertising anchors:** "Winston tastes good like a…", "How do you spell relief?" — repetition installs the link, so you can re-anchor old phrases.
- **Money "grows out of the positive ideas I follow through on every single day."**
- "Webster's Dictionary is *not* the result of an explosion in a print factory."
- His wealth affirmation: *"God's wealth is circulating in my life. His wealth flows to me in avalanches of abundance. All my needs, desires, and goals are met instantaneously."*
- A dime out of a dollar vs. $100,000 out of a million — "if you don't develop the habits now, you won't do it when the numbers get bigger."

## The Day 18 exercise

Robbins runs several writing tasks in your Success Journal, then sets a 10-day challenge:

1. **Write the pain of lack.** List everything it's costing you *right now* to not have financial abundance — what you're missing, who you can't help (a sick relative, grandparents, a child's school), the "someday, honey" promises. Make it visceral so your brain says "I've had it."
2. **Free-associate to money.** For ~60 seconds, write the single words that pop up when you think of "money" / "a lot of money." (Often mostly *positive*.)
3. **Write what you were taught growing up.** For ~2 minutes, jot the money phrases you heard explicitly and implicitly ("money is the root of all evil," "money doesn't grow on trees," "we can't afford it," "take care of the pennies…"). Notice the **conflict** between #2 and #3 — that contradiction is the sabotage.
4. **List the pleasures of abundance.** For ~120 seconds, write all the ways your life would be enhanced if money were no longer an issue — link *major pleasure* to abundance (not mere sufficiency).
5. **Rewrite every limiting belief.** Go through each phrase from #3 and either *ridicule* it or *attach an empowering new belief* (e.g., "money doesn't grow on trees — that's right, it grows out of my creative ideas acted on every day"; "it's too expensive — it used to be, but now it's an investment in my joy").
6. **The 10-Day Financial Challenge (start today):** every day for 10 days, write **three new ideas to make money** — new or existing, a service, business, investment, or invention. Quality doesn't matter; you only need one or two good ones. After 10 days you'll have 30 ideas *and* a conditioned habit of scanning for financial opportunity.

He also frames longer-term wealth conditioning: decide your **abundance number** (a "must," not sufficiency); daily condition your mind so your **reticular activating system (RAS)** filters *for* opportunity; get **role models / a vehicle** (do what you love, massively, creating value, intelligently for profit); **see yourself as a giver** — give away 10% of what you earn, use 10% to reduce debt, save/invest 10% (per *The Richest Man in Babylon*, George Clason); and create **"play money"** experiences so your brain links direct pleasure to earning.

## Make it stick — how to apply it, plus honest context

**What modern psychology backs up**

- Re-weighting the costs of inaction and benefits of action mirrors **decisional balance** and **cognitive reappraisal** — and loss aversion makes the "pain of lack" lever genuinely motivating.
- Rewriting inherited "money scripts" is well-supported: **financial psychology** research (e.g., Klontz's *money scripts*) shows beliefs absorbed in childhood predict financial behavior. Surfacing and reframing them is real work.
- The **RAS / selective attention** point is broadly accurate — priming a goal does make you notice relevant opportunities (the frequency illusion), even if it doesn't *create* them.
- "Giving increases well-being" has support; consistent generosity is linked to greater life satisfaction (though not to a guaranteed financial return).

**Where to keep your skepticism**

- **"Broke *only* because you link pain to money" is an overreach** — same caveat as Day 2. It's a sharp lens on *internal* blocks, but it sidesteps wages, debt, and structural barriers. Use it on your wiring, not as self-blame for hardship.
- **"Totally broke to a million-dollar net worth in 12 months" via an affirmation** is unverifiable, motivational framing. The durable part is the *conditioning habit*, not the timeline or the metaphysics.
- **"Like attracts like" / "avalanches of abundance"** is law-of-attraction language. Treat the affirmation as a state-and-focus tool (which Robbins concedes only works *with* real feeling and action), not a literal mechanism that pays bills.
- The "studio engineer's" association lists are illustrative anecdotes, not data.

**Apply it now:** do tasks #1 and #4 honestly — let yourself *feel* both the cost of lack and the joy of abundance — then start the 10-Day Challenge with three ideas before bed. Momentum beats perfection.
