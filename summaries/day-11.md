# Day 11 · The Power of Goal Setting

> 📄 **[Read the full cleaned transcript →](../transcripts/day-11.md)**  ·  🏠 **[Home](../README.md)**

**Core message:** With goals, you **create your future in advance** — but the secret isn't the *what*, it's the *why*. **Purpose is stronger than outcome:** a big enough reason (and enough self-imposed pressure) will pull you toward goals that today look impossible. The real prize isn't the things you get; it's *who you become* on the way.

## The big ideas

- **Goals create the future in advance.** We all have goals whether we know it or not — the trouble is most people's are lousy ("get through the day," "pay the bills"), and those don't make you jump out of bed inspired.
- **Don't get caught in the law of familiarity.** You've heard "set goals" so often you take it for granted — but *fundamentals must be practiced daily*. Lombardi, John Wooden (400 free throws, 11 straight national titles), and Robbins' Taekwondo teacher Grand Master Jhoon Rhee (a black belt is just **seven simple moves** mastered over and over) all coached fundamentals. *"Repetition is the mother of skill."* Do the exercise as if brand new.
- **Purpose > outcome.** The reason to set a goal is *what it makes of you*. At the end of life, your stuff won't make you happy — only who you've become and the moments you'll never forget. Chasing only "things" can cost you your integrity.
- **Reasons come first, answers come second.** Get a big enough *why* and you'll figure out *how*. Don't ask "how do I do it?" first — *get it down* first.
- **Why goals work (Robbins' theories):** (1) **Thoughts are things** — whatever you focus on consistently, you experience. (2) Setting a goal makes your mind notice the **gap** between where you are and where you want to be, which breeds productive **dissatisfaction**.
- **Use pressure on purpose.** Pressure and tension drive nearly all human action (hunger, etc.). Dissatisfaction is *power*, not something to hide from. *"Pressure is how diamonds are made."* Commit publicly so you can't back out.
- **Carrot AND stick.** Write the positive *whys* (what the goal gives you) *and* the "negative" whys (what it'll **cost you** if you don't achieve it) — link pleasure to attaining it, pain to missing it.
- **Goals stay flexible.** Reevaluate; you can change a goal anytime. Set goals at least twice a year, review them monthly in detail, and review your top goals daily.

## Memorable lines &amp; metaphors

- **"Purpose is stronger than outcome."**
- **"Reasons come first, answers come second."**
- **"Don't get real — get intelligent."** What's "real" to you is built from your *past*; don't let it cap your future.
- **"Repetition is the mother of skill."**
- **Pressure makes diamonds** — "just rocks that got under intense pressure."
- **"When people succeed, they party; when they fail, they ponder"** — and the pondering is where the distinctions come from.
- *Desire* in Latin means "**of the Father**" — the moment you can desire a goal, the ability to achieve it comes with it.

## The Day 11 exercise

Pull out your **Success Journal** and write down two things:

1. **What are the areas of your life you're dissatisfied with?** Be honest (not down on yourself) — scan yourself **physically, emotionally, spiritually, intellectually, financially, and in your relationships**. Ask: *"What's not good in my life? What am I not satisfied with?"* Make a solid list — that dissatisfaction creates the pressure to set powerful new goals.
2. **What beliefs would you need?** Write two or three **new beliefs** you'd have to live by in order to actually set your goals and achieve them.

Leave today focused on getting **big enough *why*s** — then tomorrow you'll do the full goal-setting workshop. (He suggests re-listening to this tape first so you arrive inspired.)

## Make it stick — how to apply it, plus honest context

**What modern psychology backs up**

- Writing goals down and reviewing them maps onto research on **implementation intentions** and the well-documented edge of **specific, written goals** over vague intentions.
- The "notice the gap → feel dissatisfied → take action" dynamic is essentially **goal-discrepancy / self-discrepancy theory** — a real driver of motivation.
- Linking pleasure to success and pain to inaction is **associative learning**, and the carrot/stick framing echoes legitimate motivation work.
- Public commitment increasing follow-through is supported by research on the **commitment / consistency** principle.
- "Purpose over things" lines up with findings that **intrinsic, purpose-driven goals** sustain well-being better than purely material ones.

**Where to keep your skepticism**

- The **1953 Yale "3% with written goals"** study is a famous example — and it's almost certainly **apocryphal**. Researchers have never been able to locate it; treat it as a parable, not data.
- Robbins' own arc — broke and overweight to **80% of his goals and a $1M+ net worth in under 12 months**, attracting his dream spouse, a stretch limo in six months, a top-three resort in Fiji — is **unverifiable, extraordinary, and motivational sales framing.** Inspiring, not a timeline.
- "Thoughts are things / write it down and it manifests" is presented as a near-mystical law ("we don't totally understand how it works"). The grounded mechanism is **focus + action + review**; don't expect manifestation without the work.

**Apply it now:** be ruthlessly specific in the dissatisfaction list and let yourself *feel* the gap — that emotion is the fuel. Then draft the two or three beliefs you'd need; you're priming the engine for tomorrow's workshop.

**Looking ahead:** Tomorrow (Part 12) is the **goal-setting workshop itself** — first identifying goals from the frame "we can achieve anything as soon as we think of it," then drilling the **why** behind each, then the **how** to make it happen.
