# Day 25 · The Final Breakthrough — Solving Problems Fast

> 📄 **[Read the full cleaned transcript →](../transcripts/day-25.md)**  ·  🏠 **[Home](../README.md)**

**Core message:** No matter how skilled you get, problems *will* come up — they're part of life, and their purpose is to make you grow. The one final ability you need is to handle them quickly: **spend 80% of your time on the solution and only 20% on the problem**, stay out of overwhelm, and ask better questions. Master that, and challenges stop devastating you and start expanding you.

## The big ideas

- **Problems are inevitable and *useful*.** They're "part of what allow us to grow and expand and become somebody." Without them, life would be boring and you'd never find out who you really are.
- **The 80/20 rule of problem solving:** spend 80% of your time on the *solution*, only 20% on the problem. What turns little problems into "big monsters" is going over and over what's wrong.
- **Questions make or break you.** "Why does this always happen to me?" are *endless-loop* questions that whip you into a frenzy. Better: "How can I turn this around — fast — and maybe even have fun doing it?"
- **Overwhelm is the real enemy.** It squashes your inherent ability to deal with anything. Beat it by linking *massive pain* to being overwhelmed and *pleasure* to solving problems — some of life's greatest satisfaction comes from pulling off what looked impossible.
- **Manage your state first.** You've turned around the impossible before; trust yourself. The way is never found by giving up or going into overwhelm.
- **It's come full circle** — back to Day 1's **Ultimate Success Formula** (know your outcome, act, notice, change approach) plus **modeling**: someone has already solved your problem; go get their answers. Human challenges are common, and so are the solutions.
- **Reframe "problem" as "challenge."** The words we use create states in us. Problems feel okay; *challenges* are inspirational — "I love a challenge."
- **The purpose of living is to expand**, and problems are what cause it. (Robbins' own proof: systematically turning his company back from roughly **$758,000 in debt** after a man embezzled a quarter-million — which transformed his belief about what he was capable of.)

## Memorable lines & metaphors

- **"Spend 80% of your time on the solution and only 20% on the problem."**
- **"Momentum carries with it power, and inertia binds you to stagnation."**
- **"What would happen if…?"** — the flexibility question that opens possibilities instead of shutting them off with "no, that'll never work."
- **The expanding circle:** you're an X at the center; your circle is your influence — what you can easily handle. A problem ("a big giant X") smacks just outside it. Most people rush to the edge, hit their outer limits, get scared, and retreat — so *another* problem soon shows up. Keep hitting the wall until enough pain makes you **break through**, and your **whole circle gets bigger** — what was once beyond reach is now within it. "The purpose of problems is to cause you to expand."
- **"To throw forth"** — the supposed root meaning of the word *problem*. The playful implication: maybe *you* throw these challenges out there so you'll keep growing. (Robbins admits he doesn't know if it's true — just "a fun and useful way of looking at problems.")
- **The Jacuzzi calls:** burnt out after ~25 seminars in ~27–28 days, he came home for only 48 hours to nearly 100 personal messages and "went nuts" — until five questions took him from overwhelm to making every call from his hot tub, teasing people and "having a blast."

## The Day 25 exercise

1. **Jot down the five problem-solving questions** (replay the tape if you miss any):
   1. **What's great about this?** (If the answer is "nothing" — *what could be great about it?*)
   2. **What's not perfect *yet*?** (Presupposes it's going to be perfect.)
   3. **What am I willing to do to make it the way I want it?**
   4. **What am I willing to *no longer* do in order to make things the way I want them?**
   5. **How can I do what's necessary to get this job done *and enjoy the process*?**
2. **Pull a real problem out of your life** — something that's a challenge right now.
3. **Run yourself through all five questions and *write down* the answers** — don't just answer them verbally.

The promise: you'll not only solve the problem, you may create a new opportunity for a lot more fun. "Play with your problems. Enjoy them and grow from them."

## Make it stick — how to apply it, plus honest context

**What modern psychology backs up**

- The 80/20 focus shift is **solution-focused therapy** in a sentence — and the rumination warning ("why does this always happen?") matches research that **brooding rumination** worsens mood without solving anything.
- The five questions are textbook **cognitive reappraisal**, plus the presupposition trick that quietly reframes a threat as solvable — a legitimate emotion-regulation move.
- "Try, notice, change your approach" is again **deliberate practice / behavioral experiments**, and modeling echoes **Bandura's social learning theory**.
- Linking pleasure to mastering hard things tracks with **self-efficacy** — competence built through earned wins genuinely transforms what you believe you can do.

**Where to keep your skepticism**

- The **circle metaphor** and "we throw forth our own problems" are *useful frames, not literal mechanisms.* Robbins says so himself. Don't twist "you create your problems" into self-blame for hardship that's structural, medical, or just bad luck.
- The **etymology** is shaky — "problem" comes from Greek *probléma* (something put *before* you), so "to throw forth" is closer to poetic license than settled fact. Enjoy it; don't quote it as linguistics.
- The embezzlement / debt-turnaround figures are **a personal anecdote** (and the transcript's number is garbled) — inspiring, but unverifiable. Take it as motivation, not a case study.
- "This system will work for *anything*" is motivational absolutism. The five questions are a strong *state-changer*; they won't dissolve every real-world constraint on their own.

**Apply it now:** take the problem nagging you most and answer all five questions *on paper*. Notice how question 2 ("not perfect *yet*") and question 5 ("…and enjoy the process") shift your state before you've even chosen a tactic.

**Looking ahead:** this is billed as "the final breakthrough," but not the final day — Robbins signs off, "I look forward to being with you again tomorrow. Till then, live with passion."
