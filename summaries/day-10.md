# Day 10 · Changing Core Beliefs — The Dickens Pattern

> 📄 **[Read the full cleaned transcript →](../transcripts/day-10.md)**  ·  🏠 **[Home](../README.md)**

**Core message:** Beliefs are just generalizations you've made — and you can change them as easily as editing files on a computer *once you know how*. The method is the same Neuro-Associative Conditioning you already learned: identify a limiting belief, link **massive pain** to keeping it, install an empowering replacement, and link **massive pleasure** to the new one until it sticks. The featured tool is the **Dickens pattern** — Tony's name for the *A Christmas Carol* technique of dragging your past, present, and future pain into the room until your brain says "never again."

## The big ideas

- **A turning point in the program.** Tony announces a shift: the foundation is laid, and from here some tapes become **built-in workshops** — you'll do the work *during* the session, not just at the end.
- **The 10-day recap.** He re-walks the whole arc: personal power, the Ultimate Success Formula (outcome → action → notice → flexibility), modeling, pain and pleasure as the controlling forces, anchors/neuro-associations, states (physiology + focus), the power of questions, and values & beliefs.
- **Beliefs run through the same 3 keys as any change:** (1) get **leverage**, (2) **interrupt the old pattern**, (3) **condition a new association.**
- **The 5-step belief-change process:** identify the limiting belief → link pain to it → create an empowering replacement → link pleasure to the new one → condition it by repetition.
- **Don't judge beliefs by accuracy — judge them by usefulness.** Nearly every belief has an exception, so "as long as we're making things up, make up ones that empower you." Tony admits beliefs he held five years ago now embarrass him.
- **Beliefs are generalizations.** "I'm too young / too old" is *too young/old for what?* He turned **"I'm too young" into "youth is power"** (youth = energy and focus, not age).
- **The Dickens pattern.** Scrooge had no desire to change and could "prove" his meanness made him rich — yet three "neuro-associative conditioning specialists" changed him in one night by making him *experience* (not be told) pain across past, present, and future, then offering a new association (giving = pleasure).
- **Linking enough pain does double duty:** it creates leverage *and* interrupts the pattern simultaneously, leaving a **vacuum** the new belief can fill.

## Memorable lines & metaphors

- **"Changing your beliefs is as easy as going on a computer and changing files."** — easy once you know how; impossible if you don't.
- **"You can't teach an old dog new tricks?"** — you can, if you "get out a big stick and a big carrot." Some people just need a bigger stick.
- **"The past does not equal the future."** — Edison "failed" ~9,999 times at the light bulb; the past is a reference, not a verdict.
- **"There is always a way — if I'm committed."** — when $250k was embezzled and his company was **$758,000 in debt**, everyone asked *which bankruptcy?*; he asked *what do we need to do to make this work?*
- **Colonel Sanders** built an empire after age 65 — proof that "I'm too old" is just a generalization.
- "Power is use, not just knowledge."

## The Day 10 exercise

This is a "do it now" workshop. The Dickens pattern works best stopped/parked or at home — *not* while focused on driving.

1. **Identify two core limiting beliefs** holding your life back. Write each as a single sentence (e.g., "I'm not smart enough," "I don't have enough money"). Put them in your Success Journal.
2. **Run the pain side of Dickens.** Close your eyes; feel the *weight* of these beliefs. Honestly feel what they've cost you — relationships, finances, self-esteem, energy, role-modeling, spirituality.
3. **Drag the pain into the future**, stacking it like a growing snowball: step **5, then 10, then 20 years** ahead still living these beliefs. At each mile-marker, look in the mirror and feel the price you've paid. Decide: *am I willing to make this my destiny?*
4. **Come back to today** — none of it has happened yet. Shake out, change your physiology, and break the state completely.
5. **Get into a peak, excited state** and write **two new empowering beliefs** (the antithesis of the old ones), stated as positive single sentences.
6. **Run the pleasure side of Dickens.** Step **5, 10, and 20 years** into a *new* destiny living the new beliefs; feel the gains across every area. Then view **both destinies side by side** and choose.
7. **Today's only written assignment:** in your journal, write **2–3 sentences per new belief** on exactly how each one will enhance the quality of your life.

## Make it stick — how to apply it, plus honest context

**What modern psychology backs up**

- The whole sequence is **counter-conditioning / associative learning** — Tony even invokes **Pavlov** by name. Pairing a cue with a new emotional response, then rehearsing it, is real.
- Steps 2–3 are essentially **playing the tape forward** and CBT's **cost–benefit / decisional balance** work — vividly imagining future consequences to shift present motivation.
- "Beliefs are generalizations; judge them by usefulness, not accuracy" lines up with **cognitive reframing** and the pragmatic spirit of CBT.
- Replacing a belief and reinforcing it through repetition mirrors **mental rehearsal** and habit formation.
- "Past ≠ future" is a clean statement of a **growth mindset** and personal agency.

**Where to keep your skepticism**

- **"There is always a way if you're committed"** is an explicit **overgeneralization** — Tony himself admits "this belief isn't accurate all the time." It's a useful operating stance, not a law; commitment doesn't dissolve every structural or external constraint.
- The dramatic anecdotes — the **$758,000 debt / quarter-million embezzlement** turnaround — are **unverifiable** personal stories. Take them as motivation, not proof.
- Edison's "9,999 attempts" and the Colonel Sanders / Pavlov references are **motivational shorthand** (the exact figures are folklore). The underlying point still stands.
- "Change a belief as easily as editing computer files" undersells how stubborn deep beliefs (especially trauma-linked ones) can be. A guided 8-minute visualization can spark change; lasting change usually needs **repetition** — which, to his credit, is step five.

**Apply it now:** the Dickens pattern only works if you *actually feel it* — Tony repeats "feel it, don't just think it" because intellectual review won't move your brain. Be specific about real relationships and real costs, and let the emotion be uncomfortable; that discomfort is the leverage.

**Looking ahead:** Tomorrow moves from dismantling old beliefs to building forward — **creating a compelling future and understanding the power of *why*.**
