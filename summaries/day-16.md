# Day 16 · Anchoring — Buttons to Your Own Brain

> 📄 **[Read the full cleaned transcript →](../transcripts/day-16.md)**  ·  🏠 **[Home](../README.md)**

**Core message:** You're *always* anchoring — your brain links emotional states to whatever's around when you feel them, whether you mean to or not. The skill isn't to start anchoring; it's to take **conscious control** of it: install the states you want on a trigger you choose, kill the negative ones, and condition yourself to move toward your goals automatically. The goal is to have **buttons to your own brain** so you can shift from frustrated to excited, or angry to laughing, in a moment.

## The big ideas

- **You're already anchoring.** Your nervous system is constantly making neuro-associations and creating meaning. Tony's job isn't to teach it — it's to make you *aware* so you can control it instead of conditioning yourself by accident.
- **How an anchor forms:** any time you're in an **intense emotional state**, anything **unique** that happens **consistently** while you're in that state gets linked to it. (Pavlov's dogs: hunger + repeated bell → the bell alone triggers the state.)
- **Negative anchors are real and sneaky.** The grieving man tapped on the same shoulder at a funeral can feel sudden, unexplained depression months later when touched the same way. Anchors can also be **auditory** (background music), **visual** (a certain look), or a **smell** (grandma's apple pie) — and stacked negative anchors quietly kill relationships.
- **Creating an anchor — two steps:** (1) put yourself fully into the desired emotional state; (2) at the **peak**, do something unique and consistent (clap + "yes!"), repeating with strong emotion until it conditions. You must be **associated** — feel it through your whole body.
- **Use anchors already around you.** Music, a comedian's tape before a talk, or — in business — rituals and symbols of power. Tony's people listen to Eddie Murphy to walk in laughing instead of anxious.
- **Collapsing a negative anchor:** (1) **stack a series** of strong positive anchors on a *different* spot; (2) fire the positive series and the negative **simultaneously** — the stronger collapses the weaker, leaving the old trigger neutral or even positive.
- **The swish pattern** anchors not one feeling but a *direction* — conditioning the start of an unwanted behavior to instantly trigger an image of your ideal self.
- **Behavior follows state.** Trigger the right state and the right performance follows — you don't have to consciously remember the steps.

## Memorable lines & metaphors

- **"I want you to have buttons to your own brain. You should be in charge."**
- **"Repetition is the mother of skill."**
- **The roller coaster:** set the trigger right at the *peak* of emotion — "not as you're about to hit the top or on your way down."
- **"Watch any commercial"** — advertisers are master anchorers: they pump you into a peak state, then flash the product over and over.
- **The slingshot / "whoosh!"** — pull the tiny ideal-self image back behind the big unwanted one, let go, and feel it explode through.
- **"How we behave is based on the state we're in."**

## The Day 16 exercise

Two assignments:

1. **Create one positive anchor that really works.**
   1. **Decide** on the emotion you want at your fingertips (motivation, passion, confidence, creativity, etc.).
   2. **Get back into** vivid past times when you genuinely felt that way — breathe and move the way you did, at the peak of the moment.
   3. **Link the feeling** to something unique (snap your fingers, make a fist, clap and say "yes").
   4. **Repeat over and over** with strong emotion until firing the trigger reliably produces the feeling.
2. **Do at least 15 swish patterns** taking you from **procrastination** into the person you're committed to being — someone who makes things happen and does not delay.

For the swish: (1) picture the *beginning* of the unwanted behavior, big and bright; (2) build a small, dim, inspiring image of your **ideal self**; (3) slingshot the ideal-self image so it bursts through and replaces the old one — say "whoosh!" and feel the breakthrough each time; (4) repeat fast, ~25 reps, re-running until thinking of the old behavior won't bring back the old image.

## Make it stick — how to apply it, plus honest context

**What modern psychology backs up**

- Anchoring is **classical (Pavlovian) conditioning** — the dog/bell mechanism is real, and **state-dependent / cue-triggered emotion** is well documented.
- Self-cued state change overlaps with legitimate techniques: **cue-based habit triggers** ("atomic habits"), **emotion regulation**, and **pre-performance routines** athletes genuinely use to enter a "flow" state.
- The swish pattern's logic — rehearsing a new response until it fires automatically — rhymes with **mental imagery / behavioral rehearsal**, which has real support in sport and performance psychology.

**Where to keep your skepticism**

- **Anchoring and the swish pattern are NLP techniques**, and **NLP as a system lacks robust controlled-trial evidence.** A self-anchor can help you *access* a mood you can already summon; it won't reliably install emotions you've never felt, and effects are usually modest, not magical.
- The dramatic results are **anecdotal and unverifiable** — Ken Blanchard taking "**15 strokes**" off his golf game in two days after 20–30 minutes on a plane, and the swish that *permanently* stopped a lifelong nail-biting habit. Take them as motivation, not data.
- The **"collapse a phobia in minutes"** claim is exactly where NLP outruns the evidence; durable phobia treatment is **exposure therapy**, not a shoulder-touch. The claim that *anything* unique consistently linked to a state will anchor is overstated — strength, uniqueness, timing, and intensity all matter, as Tony himself notes.
- The North / Michael Jackson / "general's chair" stories illustrate the *idea* of cues and authority influence, but they're **persuasive storytelling**, not measured proof that anchoring caused the outcomes.

**Apply it now:** pick **one** emotion and build a single, genuinely unique anchor — fire it only at the *peak* of real feeling, and repeat enough times that it sticks. Then run the procrastination swish fast and physically. Don't over-engineer it; the lever is **intensity + repetition + uniqueness.**
