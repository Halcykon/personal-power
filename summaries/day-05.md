# Day 5 · Emotion Is Created by Motion — Managing Your State

> 📄 **[Read the full cleaned transcript →](../transcripts/day-05.md)**  ·  🏠 **[Home](../README.md)**

**Core message:** Everything you really want isn't the money, the relationship, or the business — it's the **feeling** you believe those things will give you. Since all you ultimately want is to change how you feel, the master skill of life is learning to **manage your own state** (the state of mind and body you're in right now). And the fastest lever for that is your **physical body**: emotion is created by motion.

## The big ideas

- **You don't want the thing — you want the feeling.** Nobody wants "little pieces of paper with pictures of deceased notables." Keep asking "why do you want that?" and every desire bottoms out at *because it makes me feel good.* All behavior is the pursuit of a feeling.
- **Performance = state, not ability.** Why do you sometimes perform brilliantly and sometimes "brutally"? The difference isn't capability — *"What people can do is incredible; what they will do is usually disappointing."* It's the **state** you're in. Forgetting your own phone number or how to spell "the" = a temporary **"stupid state,"** not lost knowledge.
- **You — not circumstances — control your state.** How you feel is "purely designed" by how you direct your own mind and body, not by the economy, the weather, your relationships, or how others treat you. Take that responsibility and you gain control of your life at a new level.
- **The price of not managing it:** Belushi, Elvis, Freddie Prinze, Hendrix, Joplin, Marilyn Monroe — people with every reason to be happy who never learned to manage their state and tried to change how they felt with drugs. **W. Mitchell** is the contrast: burned over three-quarters of his body in a motorcycle/truck fire, later paralyzed in a plane crash, yet stayed so "up" people assumed he was on drugs — because he *decided* to manage his state, focusing on what he could do rather than what he'd lost.
- **Why before how:** ~80% of success is *why* to manage your state; 20% is *how.*
- **Two ways to control state:** (1) your **physiology** (how you use your body) and (2) your **mental focus** (today is physiology; focus is next tape).
- **Depression is something you *do*.** Picture a depressed person: slumped, head down, eyes down, shallow breathing, slack face. To get depressed "you have to work at it" — use your body a specific way. Stand tall, breathe fully, grin at the ceiling, and you literally **can't** get depressed without changing your body first.
- **Affirmations alone fail.** Chant "I feel good" while slumped and "your brain goes, BS." Change the *movement*, not just the words — "your body will lead your mind."
- **You already do this — often badly.** Alcohol, cigarettes, food, drugs all "work" because they change your biochemistry/breathing and thus your state instantly. The cigarette's real effect is the slow, deep breath — *"Why not try it without the cigarette?"*
- **Four levers of physiology:** (1) **movement** — speed/acceleration of gestures; (2) **breathing**; (3) the **face** — 80+ muscles, "facial aerobics"; (4) **diet** — blood sugar alone shifts emotion.
- **Scale of 0–10:** borrowed from athletes (the hitter facing Dwight Gooden who needs to be at "level 12"). Know where you *are* and where you *need to be* for the task — and match the state to the context (level 10 is wrong for saying "I love you").

## Memorable lines & metaphors

- **"Emotion is created by motion."**
- **"What people can do is incredible; what they will do is usually disappointing"** — the difference is state, not ability.
- The **"stupid state"** — when the answers are there but your brain can't reach them.
- **Belushi vs. W. Mitchell** — the man who had everything and quit life, vs. the man who lost almost everything and refused to.
- Mitchell's campaign button: *"Send me to the governor's mansion and I won't just be another pretty face"* — a way to interrupt people's pattern.
- **Power moves** — a signature gesture that instantly makes you feel strong, resourceful, unstoppable.
- **Facial aerobics** — run through every expression you can to gain access to every state.
- "Why not try it without the cigarette? What a concept."
- "Your body will lead your mind."

## The Day 5 exercise

Your one assignment today — best done out loud, with someone, the wall, the road, or the crowd:

1. **Talk dispassionately.** Speak about something you're *normally* inspired about, but in a flat, dispassionate, "I-don't-really-believe-it" way for two or three minutes. Notice how you stand, gesture, breathe, and use your voice (volume, tempo, tone) and face. Notice that you don't like how it feels.
2. **Talk passionately.** Now flip completely — talk about the same thing more inspired, excited, and passionate than you can ever remember, going "a thousand percent," all the way (or don't do it at all).
3. **Dissect and record.** Pull out your **Success Journal** and write down the exact differences — how you *moved*, *breathed*, used your *face*, and used your *voice* between the two states. The goal is to learn "the buttons in your brain": e.g., snapping a hand out fast, speaking faster, or leaning in triggers *your* passion.
4. **Experiment during the day.** At a moment when you feel flat, ask "Where am I, 0–10?" If you're at a 4–6, **snap yourself** into a powerful state by changing your movement — or practice going the other way, relaxing quickly via breathing/tempo. Whatever state you're weakest at entering, practice until you can make the shift as fast as you can think it.

## Make it stick — how to apply it, plus honest context

**What modern psychology backs up**

- The core claim — that **deliberately adopting posture, facial expression, and movement shifts emotion** — is real. This is the **facial-feedback hypothesis** and broader **embodied cognition**: how you hold and move your body feeds back into how you feel.
- "Go do something" to beat low mood is essentially **behavioral activation**, a well-supported treatment for depression.
- The slow, deep breathing behind the "cigarette effect" genuinely down-regulates arousal via the parasympathetic nervous system — controlled breathing is an evidence-based calming tool.
- Choosing your response to circumstances ("you control your state") echoes the **Stoic / CBT** insight that our appraisals, not events themselves, drive much of how we feel — a legitimate **emotion-regulation** stance.
- Athletes' pre-performance rituals (the tennis bounce, the routines) are real **pre-performance routines / anchoring** used in sports psychology.

**Where to keep your skepticism**

- **Posture is a lever, not a cure.** Facial-feedback effects are real but **modest**, and some famous "power posing" findings have **failed to replicate** at the dramatic size once claimed. This helps you nudge your state; it doesn't override clinical depression, trauma, or grief.
- **"You always control how you feel" is an overreach.** It's empowering, but biology, chemistry, exhaustion, and real adversity matter. Don't weaponize it into self-blame for not being able to "just smile."
- **The UCSF study on manic-depressives who "could not feel depressed while smiling"** is stated dramatically and is **unverifiable as described** — treat it as illustration, not proof. (Bipolar disorder is a serious medical condition; expression changes are no substitute for treatment.)
- **W. Mitchell is a real person and a genuinely remarkable story**, but the specifics here are Tony's retelling — inspiring, and best taken as motivation rather than a documented case file.
- The **celebrity-deaths list** is a rhetorical device; addiction and mental illness are complex, not simply a failure to "manage state."

**Apply it now:** before your next hard moment — a tough call, a conversation, motivating your kid — rate yourself 0–10, decide the state the task actually needs, then *change your body first*: stand taller, speed up your gestures, breathe and speak with more energy. Watch the feeling follow the motion.

**Looking ahead:** Day 5 is only the *physiology* half of state control. The next tape tackles the second lever — **mental focus**: the pictures you make, what you pay attention to, and what you say to yourself — and how steering those instantly changes your state too.
